  • Software Engineer

    Netuitive

    Netuitive is a predictive analytics platform that helps IT organizations avoid downtime and service-degredations before they occur. Netuitive learns the normal behavior for each component in an organization's IT infrastructure, and understands how it relates to other components. When a component deviates from normal, Netuitive can proactively alert administrators as to which parts of the infrastructure will be affected, and when.

    Technology

    The core Netuitive product is a Spring Framework application that is shipped via downloadable installers for Windows and Linux. The installer installs Tomcat, provisions the database, and deploys the application. After the initial installation, administrators can install additional packages that add features and integrate with other performance monitoring tools.

    At Netuitive, I added several reporting features to the core product, but my more interesting work was on a secondary product called Hydra. Along with two other engineers, I built hydra using Groovy and Grails that allowed customers running several instances of the core product to aggregate and view data across all instances. This product also added Single-Sign-On to the core product. I also worked on third-party integrations with NetIQ and IBM Tivoli.

    Leadership

    I think I surprised a few people with how quickly I came up to speed on what is a rather complex product. Shortly after I arrived at Netuitive, I was influencing development decisions, corresponding with third-party vendors with whom we had partnerships, and presenting my work to senior company leadership.

    I also spent several months mentoring an intern, who still works for Netuitive today.

    Entrepreneurship

    Perhaps my biggest accomplishment at Netuitive was building a well-performing integration with IBM's Tivoli. At the time, Tivioli represented about one third of the application performance management market, yet its APIs were too slow for us to effectively integrate. I used a unique approach which allowed Netuitive to effeciently read Tivoli data, opening up a whole new set of potential customers. This work positioned me to win Netuitive's "Rookie of the Year" award.

    I also cultivated a relationship with Netuitive's director of solution architecture, and supported him on difficult proof-of-concept demonstrations at customer sites.

  • Software Engineer

    Computech is a software and project management consultancy with a core competency in wireless spectrum auctions, among other areas. While the bulk of my work was done for the United States' Federal Communications Commission, Computech used what it learned at the FCC to grow its spectrum auctions business in Mexico, Canada, Ireland, and Switzerland.

    Technology

    At the Federal Communications Commission, I worked to develop new features and improve the performance of the Integrated Spectrum Auctions System and the Universal Licensing System. Together, the two systems allocate and manage the usage of all airwaves in the United States. The two systems were built on a variety of technologies: Standard JEE, the Spring Framework, and Adobe ColdFusion. All components were hosted via BEA Weblogic and ran on top of Sybase.

    Most notably, I overhauled the system that generated printed licenses and correspondence so that spectrum license holders could retrieve pixel-perfect, historically accurate PDF copies (of the original PostScript version of the document) for all correspondence from the FCC.

    Leadership

    During the transition from analog to digital television in the US, I led the development of the DTV.gov website. The site took on many different forms during the transition and afterward, but much of the underlying technology and data models remained the same due my early design work.

    I was also the first person to introduce the Scrum methodology at Computech. Introducing Scrum at a CMMI Level III organization is no walk in the park, but we managed to learn enough about how the project would have to work during the pilot project that we were able to make it a regular development methodology at the company.

    In my final year at Computech, I led a company wide transition from Yahoo Mail to Google Apps. The effort involved evaluating a number of intranet tools, building consensus among company leadership, making the technology switch, and training the 250 employees on the basics of Gmail, Google Docs, and Google Sites. Ultimately, the switch made collaboration far easier, and for my effort, I won the company's "Above and Beyond Award".

    Entrepreneurship

I could see the Cloud Computing trend emerging though, so I led an iniative to improve Computech's familiarity with Amazon Web Services, Google App Engine, and Rackspace. As a result, the company was able to speak intelligently about the tradeoffs of each, when most competitors only knew Cloud Computing as a buzz word.

  • Information Visualization Intern

    Virginia Tech, Computational Bioinformatics & Bioimaging Laboratory

    CBIL (for short) was a well-funded Virginia Tech laboratory that conducted reserearch in the area of Gene Module Discovery, using unsupervised machine learning and other statistical techniques.

    Technology

    As an intern at CBIL, I built a desktop visualization tool called ModVis that read the output files of many different module discovery algorithms and display their data in a way that allowed medical doctors to see and explore the commonalities between them. The tool itself was built using J2SE Swing with custom AWT components, and it read data generated by AffyMetrix MicroArray chips.

    Leadership

    The majority of my work at CBIL was done independently, with monthly check-ins from a senior professor. It was largely up to me to decide what techonologies to use, what to build, and how. Upon graduating from college, I helped to on-board a new graduate student who continued the work after I left.

    Entrepreneurship

    Academic research is not intrinsically entrepreneurial, but the potential impact of this work was never lost on me. If we could identify a cluster of genes, that when under- or over-expressed, cause disorders like Muscular Dystrophy or Cancer, it could be possible for medical doctors to create a 'healthy' virus that would infect the affected cells, reverse the genetic anomaly, and cure the affliction.

    In November of 2006, I published and presented my work at the 19th International Conference on Computer Applications in Industry and Engineering in Las Vegas, NV.
